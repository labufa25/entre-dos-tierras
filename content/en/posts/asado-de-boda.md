## What is Asado de Boda?

Asado de Boda is slow-braised pork in a rich, sweet-and-spicy red chile sauce — one of the handful of dishes that put Zacatecas on Mexico's culinary map. You'll find it at every big celebration: weddings, quinceañeras, baptisms.

Its preparation is considered a symbol of abundance. In the north-central region of Mexico, there is simply no wedding without it.

It's not just food. It's a tradition.


## Found Across Northern Mexico — But Every Version is Different

This dish is beloved in Durango, Coahuila, Sonora, Sinaloa, and San Luis Potosí. But every region puts its own touch on it.

Ours comes from **Fresnillo, Zacatecas** — our grandmother's recipe, unchanged.

## 120 Years of History

The roots of this dish trace back to the Mexican Revolution. According to historical accounts, General Francisco Villa arrived with his troops at the Ejido de Morelos in Zacatecas and requested a pork and chile stew to celebrate. The dish was so well received that it spread across the north, becoming a staple of every major celebration.

The name itself tells the story — *Asado de Boda* means "wedding roast." Today it represents an offering from the groom's family to honor the bride's family — a gesture of respect and commitment that has been passed down for generations.

## The Dish Itself

At its core, Asado de Boda is a slow-cooked stew. The pork — usually shoulder or loin — is braised low and slow until tender, concentrating all the flavors into something deep and layered.

The sauce is built from dried chiles, and the combination creates a flavor that is balanced between sweet and spicy, with a velvety texture that coats every piece of meat.

Pork is also a nutritional powerhouse — rich in high-quality protein, potassium, phosphorus, zinc, iron, and B vitamins including B1, B3, B6, and B12. Mexico is the 15th largest pork producer in the world, producing 1.4 million tons annually.
